# Appendix E — Exam-Day Logistics & Psychology {.type-normal}

You have trained for the League. This appendix is the part nobody studies and everybody wishes they had: what actually happens on the day you walk into the Indigo Plateau, and how to keep a prepared mind from beating itself. Knowing the room removes the surprises, and removed surprises are recovered marks. Everything here is procedure and psychology — no new math.

::: cold-open
**▶ COLD OPEN — EPISODE: "The Indigo Plateau"**

The eight badges are in your case. The mocks are behind you. This morning there is nothing left to learn — only to *perform*. The doors of the Pokémon League open at a fixed time, the proctor checks your trainer ID against a list, and a clock you do not control begins to count down. Champions are not the ones who knew the most. They are the ones who, on the one day it counted, did what they already knew how to do — calmly, in order, without panicking when a single question went sideways. That is the whole skill of exam day, and you can train it.
:::

## E.1 — Before the day: registration and the CBT format

Exam P is delivered by **computer-based testing (CBT)** through Prometric, in a testing window the SOA publishes several times a year. The logistics:

- **Register early through the SOA**, then schedule your seat at a Prometric center (or the approved live-remote-proctored option, if you take it from home). Popular dates and centers fill; booking early buys you the time slot and location you actually want.
- The exam is **3 hours** for **30 multiple-choice questions**, each with five answer choices (A–E). There is **no penalty for a wrong answer**, so you will never leave a question blank — more on that in E.5.
- Bring **government-issued photo ID** whose name exactly matches your registration. A mismatch can cost you the seat.
- Know your **center's address and travel time** in advance; arrive **30 minutes early** for check-in. Arriving late can forfeit the appointment.

::: trainers-tip
**THE WEEK-BEFORE CHECKLIST**

1. Confirm date, time, and exact center address; do a dry run of the commute if it is unfamiliar.
2. Confirm your calculator is on the approved list and **clear its memory** the night before (Appendix D, §D.1).
3. Pack: photo ID, your approved calculator, **a backup calculator of the same approved model** with fresh batteries, and your confirmation email.
4. Stop learning new material 48 hours out. The last two days are light review, sleep, and logistics — not cramming.
:::

## E.2 — The cleared-calculator rule

The SOA permits exactly one calculator from its approved list (the **TI-30XS MultiView** or **BA II Plus** are the two this book teaches). Two rules trip people up:

1. **Memory must be cleared.** A calculator with stored programs or values can be flagged. Clear it the night before and again at the desk if you are able: TI-30XS `[2nd][CLEAR]`; BA II Plus `[2nd][MEM]` then `[2nd][CLR Work]`. Display-mode settings (FLOAT, AOS) are not memory and are fine — set them as in Appendix D.
2. **Bring a backup.** Calculators die. A second unit *of an approved model* with fresh batteries is cheap insurance against the one failure that would otherwise end your exam.

The center supplies scratch material (often a laminated sheet and marker, or paper, per current Prometric policy) and you may **not** bring your own paper, notes, the formula sheet, or this book. Appendix C's normal table is provided on-screen during the exam — you will not need to bring one.

## E.3 — The CBT interface (no surprises in the room)

You sit at a workstation in a quiet, monitored room. A brief tutorial precedes the timed portion (it does **not** eat your three hours). During the exam you can:

- **Navigate freely** forward and backward among all 30 questions.
- **Flag** a question to revisit — this is the mechanical backbone of the two-pass plan (E.4).
- See a **running countdown clock**; glance at it at planned checkpoints, not constantly.
- Use an **on-screen normal-distribution table** and basic tools provided by the interface.

When you finish, you submit, and the **preliminary pass/fail result appears on screen** at the center, with the official result and any numeric score following from the SOA later. Knowing the result is immediate keeps the post-exam imagination from running wild.

::: team-rocket
**TRANSMISSION INTERCEPTED — "I'll just answer them in order and turn it in."**

Team Rocket sat down, ground through question 1 to 30 in strict order, spent eleven minutes stuck on a hard joint-density problem at number 7, and ran out of clock with four easy questions on the multivariate topic never even seen. They failed by two marks they had absolutely earned. **Order is a choice, and the wrong choice loses marks you already own.** Use the two-pass plan.
:::

## E.4 — The two-pass plan (your pacing system)

Thirty questions in 180 minutes is **6 minutes per question** on average — but the average is a trap, because questions are not equally hard. The two-pass plan spends time where it pays.

**Pass 1 — harvest the certain marks (target ~90 minutes).**
Go through all 30 questions once. For each, make a snap call in the first 30 seconds:

- *I can do this fast* → do it now, fully, and move on.
- *I can do this but it will take a while* → **flag it** and move on.
- *I don't recognize the path* → put down your best guess **now** (no blanks — there's no penalty), flag it, and move on.

By the end of Pass 1 you have banked every quick mark, you know exactly how many hard ones remain, and no flagged question is sitting blank.

**Pass 2 — spend the rest on the flagged questions (remaining ~80 minutes).**
Return to the flagged set, hardest-value first. Now you have time and no fear of leaving easy marks behind. Update guesses as you solve; if a question still won't yield after a few minutes, lock in the best guess and move on — a defended guess beats an unfinished derivation.

**The clock checkpoints.** At the **90-minute mark** you should have finished Pass 1. At **30 minutes left**, stop deep work, make sure **every question has an answer selected**, and use remaining time to recheck flagged ones. At **5 minutes left**, stop optimizing and verify nothing is blank.

::: pokedex-entry
**POKÉDEX ENTRY — The two-pass rhythm**

$$\underbrace{\text{Pass 1}}_{\sim 90\text{ min}}\;\big(\text{answer easy, flag + guess hard}\big)\;\longrightarrow\;\underbrace{\text{Pass 2}}_{\sim 80\text{ min}}\;\big(\text{work flagged, hardest-value first}\big)\;\longrightarrow\;\underbrace{\text{Final sweep}}_{\text{last 5 min}}\;\big(\text{no blanks}\big)$$

*Recognition cue:* the moment you feel a question fighting you for more than ~90 seconds on Pass 1 — flag, guess, move. Time is the resource you are managing, not pride.
:::

## E.5 — Guessing and elimination (the no-penalty edge)

Because wrong answers cost nothing, **never submit a blank** and never spend the final minutes on anything but ensuring all 30 are filled. A blind guess among five choices is worth $0.2$ of a mark in expectation; eliminating even one wrong choice raises that. Use the structure of the answers:

- **Eliminate impossible values.** A probability outside $[0,1]$, a negative variance, an "expected payment" larger than the policy limit — strike them.
- **Check the order of magnitude.** If your estimate says "around $0.3$" and only one choice is near there, that is your guess even if you cannot finish the algebra.
- **Watch for the deductible trap.** Several distractors are built from the wrong loss-vs-payment quantity (Chapter 10); if a choice equals $\E[X]$ when the question asked for $\E[(X-d)_+]$, it is the trap, not the answer.

## E.6 — Psychology: keeping a prepared mind calm

Your preparation is real; the only thing left to manage is the nervous system that can hide it from you.

- **Sleep beats cramming.** A full night's sleep before the exam recovers more marks than the last three hours of review ever could. Light review and an early night.
- **The first hard question is a feature, not a verdict.** When (not if) a question stumps you early, that is your cue to flag-and-move — it says nothing about how the other 29 will go. Candidates fail by *spiraling* on one bad question, not by missing it.
- **Box-breathe to reset.** If your heart rate spikes, four seconds in, hold four, four out, hold four — two cycles. It is faster than re-reading a question through a fog of adrenaline.
- **Anchor to your pacing checkpoints, not to other test-takers.** Someone leaving early means nothing. Your clock is your clock.
- **Pre-decide the rules** so you are not negotiating with yourself under stress: *flag at 90 seconds, never leave a blank, stop deep work at 30 minutes left.* Rules decided in advance are rules you actually follow.

::: kanto-realworld
**FROM KANTO TO THE REAL WORLD**

Every practicing actuary has sat exactly this kind of timed, high-stakes exam — the credentialing path is built on them. The skills you are training today are not test artifacts: managing a fixed time budget against tasks of uneven difficulty, refusing to let one stubborn problem sink the whole batch, and trusting prepared judgment under pressure are the daily reality of pricing, reserving, and reporting work on deadline. Exam day is the first professional simulation of the job.
:::

## E.7 — The day-of timeline

::: trainers-tip
**EXAM-DAY RUNSHEET**

- **Night before:** clear both calculators; lay out ID, calculators, confirmation; sleep early.
- **Morning:** eat normally; arrive at the center **30 minutes early**.
- **Check-in:** photo ID matched to registration; store belongings as directed; calculators verified.
- **Tutorial:** untimed — use it to settle and confirm the flag and navigation controls work.
- **Pass 1 (~90 min):** answer the easy, flag-and-guess the hard. No blanks.
- **Pass 2 (~80 min):** flagged questions, hardest-value first.
- **Last 5 min:** confirm all 30 answered; submit.
- **After:** read the preliminary on-screen result, then go celebrate the eighth badge.
:::

*Exam format details (timing, question count, CBT delivery, approved calculators, on-screen normal table) reflect the SOA Exam P syllabus as of 2026; always reconfirm the current rules on the SOA's official Exam P page when you register, as administrative details can change between sittings.*
