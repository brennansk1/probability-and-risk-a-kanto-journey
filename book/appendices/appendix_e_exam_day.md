# Appendix E — The Indigo Plateau: Exam-Day Field Guide {.type-dragon}

> **What this is.** Every practical fact and strategic habit you need for the morning of Exam P — from the moment you pack your bag the night before to the moment you submit your final answer. Nothing here requires you to learn new mathematics; this appendix is logistics and execution. Read it once during your final study week, skim the pacing table the morning of the exam, and then trust your preparation. You have earned this.

---

## 1. The Format — Know Your Arena

The Indigo Plateau has a fixed layout. Knowing it in advance means no surprises cost you time.

**Scope and structure.** SOA Exam P is **30 multiple-choice questions** with five answer options each (labeled **A through E**). You have **3 hours — 180 minutes** — to answer all 30. The questions are delivered on a computer screen at a Prometric testing center; you cannot receive a paper booklet or print anything out.

**Scoring.** Your raw score is your count of correct answers, mapped to a scale from **0 to 10**. A **scaled score of 6 is a pass**. The SOA does not publish the exact raw-to-scaled conversion for any single sitting, because it varies slightly between administrations (a process called equating, which adjusts for differences in question difficulty across sittings). What this means in practice: a raw score of 24 or more out of 30 puts you solidly in passing territory by any recent administration's curve, which is why 24/30 is the readiness gate in this book (Appendix H / ch19).

**No penalty for a wrong answer.** A blank and a wrong answer are both zero points. This has one firm implication: **never leave a question blank.** If you reach the end of time and have unanswered questions, guess — letter B or C are fine defaults, though any letter is equally valid. One-in-five odds beat zero odds every time.

**Registration and fees.** Exam P uses **continuous registration** — there is no single enrollment window or fixed sitting dates. You register through the SOA's website (soa.org), choose a Prometric testing window and location, and pay the exam fee at registration. The Tier-1 fee (for candidates who have not passed this exam) is approximately **\$275** at the time of writing; check the SOA website for the current amount. You can schedule or reschedule up to a few days before your chosen date, subject to seat availability and the SOA's rescheduling policy.

**Typical preparation.** The SOA's own guidance suggests **roughly 300 study hours** as a reasonable preparation estimate for Exam P, though the actual number varies widely by mathematical background. Candidates with strong calculus and probability foundations often need fewer hours; candidates who need to rebuild fundamentals from the ground up may need more. This book is calibrated to cover the full syllabus in those 300 hours, including problems and mock exams.

**The normal table.** The SOA provides a standard normal cumulative table on-screen during the exam — the same table reproduced in Appendix C of this book. You cannot bring your own reference sheets, tables, or notes. The normal table is the only reference material provided; everything else must come from memory and from your calculator.

## 2. Calculator Rules — Your Pikachu Must Pass Inspection

Every trainer enters the arena with a registered partner. Your calculator is that partner, and it must meet the SOA's rules exactly.

**Approved models only.** The SOA maintains a list of approved calculator models; only calculators on that list may be used. As of this writing the list includes the TI-30X family, the BA II Plus family, and a handful of others. Unapproved calculators are confiscated at check-in — the proctor does not negotiate. This book teaches the **TI-30XS MultiView** (see Appendix D), which is on the approved list and is, in this author's judgment, the machine best suited to Exam P's problem types.

**Bring two units.** Calculator failure during the exam is not grounds for extra time. If your calculator stops working and you have no backup, you finish with mental arithmetic. The solution is simple: **bring a second TI-30XS MultiView to the testing center**. Keep both in your bag. You are permitted to have a backup calculator at your workstation.

**Memory must be cleared.** Prometric proctors are required to verify that your calculator's memory has been cleared before you enter the testing room. This is not a suggestion; it is a rule. Clear both units the evening before the exam. On the TI-30XS, clearing stored variables is a two-key sequence: [2nd]{.kbd} [clear var]{.kbd}. Do this, then confirm that the memory slots hold nothing — press [2nd]{.kbd} [recall]{.kbd} on a variable and verify it reads zero. The display settings you choose (MathPrint, Float) are *not* memory and do not need to be reset; those survive the clear and are fine.

Think of the cleared-memory ritual as the last step in your evening preparation — as natural as setting your alarm. Do it, confirm it, and move on.

## 3. The Two-Pass Plan — Strategy for 30 Questions

Thirty questions in 180 minutes is 6 minutes per question on average. But the questions are not equally distributed across difficulty, and your recognition of each question's archetype is not equally fast across every topic. The two-pass plan exploits that variation.

**Pass 1 — Bank the immediate wins.** On the first pass, work through every question in order at a brisk pace. When you encounter a question whose archetype you recognize immediately — you see it, you know the setup, you start solving — work it fully and mark your answer. When you encounter a question where your first read doesn't produce an immediate plan, **flag it and move on without spending more than 30 seconds**. The goal of Pass 1 is to collect every question you can answer quickly and leave nothing on the table. A typical first pass takes roughly 60–90 minutes for a well-prepared candidate.

**Pass 2 — Return to the flagged questions.** Once you have banked the easy and medium questions, return to your flagged items with whatever time remains. You now know roughly how many questions are left and how much time you have — use that information to pace yourself through the flags. If you work a flagged question and still cannot reach a confident answer after 3–4 minutes, make your best guess from the available options and move on rather than letting one question drain time from the rest.

**Before time runs out — guess every blank.** In the final two minutes of the exam, scan for any unanswered questions. Answer every one of them, even if you have to guess blindly. A guessed answer has positive expected value; a blank has zero. Make it a rule: the exam ends with no blanks.

The CBT interface on Prometric's system lets you flag questions for review and navigate between them freely — use those features. The navigation panel also shows you which questions are answered and which are not, so you can confirm at the end that nothing is blank.

## 4. Pacing — The Clock Is a Tool, Not an Enemy

The single biggest source of exam-day underperformance is clock management. Candidates who run out of time on Question 27 do not fail because they don't know the material — they fail because they spent 15 minutes on Question 8. The pacing table below is your checkpoint system.

**The 6-minute budget.** At an even pace:

| By the end of... | You should be finishing around... |
|---|---|
| Minute 30 | Question 5 |
| Minute 60 | Question 10 |
| Minute 90 | Question 15 (halfway) |
| Minute 120 | Question 20 |
| Minute 150 | Question 25 |
| Minute 175 | Question 30 (5 min buffer) |

This table does not mean you must spend exactly 6 minutes on every question — some will take 2 minutes and some will take 9 — but if you glance at the clock and you are meaningfully behind (say, at minute 90 and only on Question 11), it is time to pick up the pace. The two-pass strategy helps because flagging and moving on keeps you from losing 12 minutes to a single question on Pass 1.

**The 5-minute buffer.** Finishing Question 30 at minute 175 leaves 5 minutes to review flagged answers, confirm no blanks, and submit. Do not sacrifice this buffer by grinding on a single question.

## 5. Sanity-Check Reflexes — Your Built-In Error Catcher

Before you confirm an answer, run it through these four checks. Each takes about 3 seconds and catches a class of errors that pure computation misses.

**Probabilities live in [0, 1].** Any answer that is negative, or greater than 1, is impossible for a probability. If your computed probability is 1.43 or −0.07, you made an arithmetic error, not just an unlikely-looking answer — go back.

**Variances are non-negative.** Variance is an average squared deviation; it cannot be negative. A computed variance of −5 means you subtracted in the wrong order somewhere.

**Expected payment cannot exceed expected loss.** In an insurance or deductible problem, the expected payment to the insured is always at most the expected loss: $\E[\text{payment}] \le \E[\text{loss}]$. Deductibles reduce the payment, limits reduce it further. If your expected payment is larger than your expected loss, the signs on your deductible or limit calculation are wrong.

**The support must be consistent.** If a distribution has support on $[0, \infty)$ and your answer involves a probability at a negative value, something has gone wrong. If a discrete variable takes values $\{1, 2, 3, 4\}$ and you are computing $P(X = 0)$ from a formula that gives a positive value, check whether you are using the right PMF.

These checks are not foolproof, but they catch the most common classes of computational error quickly. Make them a reflex, not an afterthought.

## 6. The Week, Night, and Morning Before

**The final week.** Use this week for consolidation, not new learning. Work through mock exams from Appendix H and ch19 under timed conditions. Review your error log — the problems you got wrong, and the root-cause category of each error (wrong setup, arithmetic slip, wrong archetype, ran out of time). Do not introduce new material in the final week; you want to sharpen what you have, not add half-learned concepts that create doubt under pressure.

**The night before.** Clear both calculators (the [2nd]{.kbd} [clear var]{.kbd} ritual, confirmed). Lay out everything you are bringing: your government-issued photo ID (a driver's license or passport — the proctor checks it against your registration name), both calculators, and nothing else of substance (phones, study materials, and food stay in the locker). Go to sleep at a reasonable hour. Reviewing formulas at midnight rarely moves the needle on a cumulative-learning exam; a clear head in the morning does.

**The morning of.** Eat something. Arrive at the Prometric center **at least 15 minutes early** — check-in includes ID verification, a palm-vein or fingerprint scan (depending on the center), and a locker assignment. Being late to check-in is not a recoverable situation. Once you are checked in, the proctor will inspect your calculators and show you to your workstation.

**At the workstation.** Prometric centers provide **scratch paper** (typically a laminated whiteboard sheet and a marker, or paper provided by the proctor) — you cannot bring your own. Request it before you start if it is not already at your station. The CBT interface includes a basic on-screen calculator; ignore it and use your TI-30XS. The on-screen normal table (matching Appendix C) is accessible through an exhibit button — know where it is before you start the clock.

Take a slow breath before you click Start. You have been preparing for this.

## 7. Readiness Gates — The Honest Go/No-Go Decision

No one benefits from sitting an exam they are not ready for. The following gates are the honest test:

::: trainers-tip
**Gate 1 — Mock exam performance.** Score at least **24 out of 30** on the book's mock exams (Appendix H / ch19) under timed conditions (180 minutes, no interruptions, closed book except the normal table). One score of 24 can be a good day; two or three consistent scores at 24 or above mean your underlying skill level is there. If you are consistently scoring 18–21, you have identified weaknesses to address before registering.

**Gate 2 — Coaching Actuaries Earned Level.** If you are using Coaching Actuaries' Adapt platform alongside this book, an **Earned Level of 7 or higher** indicates readiness. Earned Level is a dynamic difficulty measure that rises as you correctly answer harder questions; a 7+ means you are answering questions at, or above, the difficulty distribution of the real exam.

**Gate 3 — Six-minute pacing.** You can sustain **6-minute average pacing** across a full 30-question sitting without running out of time. If you consistently finish all 30 questions under 180 minutes with time to review, your pacing is ready. If you consistently reach Question 25 with 5 minutes left, you need either faster recognition or more disciplined flagging.

Meeting all three gates does not guarantee a 10. But it means the probability is firmly on your side — and putting probability firmly on your side is the entire point of everything in this book.
:::

## 8. The Champion's Mindset

The Indigo Plateau in the anime is not a finishing line — it is a proving ground. Ash does not arrive there having mastered everything perfectly; he arrives having worked through every gym, every setback, every rival encounter, and every lesson the road had to teach him. The Plateau is where all of that accumulates into something real.

Your exam day is the same moment. You have worked through conditional probability and discrete moments and calculus and joint distributions and order statistics. You have made errors in practice, found the root cause, and corrected them. You have timed yourself and found your pace. You know the two-pass plan, the sanity checks, and the six-minute budget.

On exam day, none of that changes. The questions are the same probability theory you have been practicing. The calculator is the same machine you have cleared and rehearsed. The time limit is the same 180 minutes you have trained against.

When a question looks unfamiliar, recognize the archetype underneath it — every question on Exam P is built from a finite set of structures you have seen. When the clock feels fast, trust your pass-one discipline and flag rather than freeze. When you finish, leave the testing center knowing that you executed the strategy you prepared.

The score comes later. Your preparation is already done.

*Good luck, Trainer. The League awaits.*
