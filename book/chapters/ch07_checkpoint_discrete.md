## Where You Are — Rank, Badges, and What This Night Is

**Rank: Ace Trainer · Badges: 5 (Boulder · Cascade · Thunder · Rainbow · Soul).** You have crossed all six legs of Act I — the *discrete* world, where every random quantity lives on whole numbers you can list and count. This is a **checkpoint**, not a chapter: there is **no new theory**, no concept, no gym, no badge. Its only job is **spaced retrieval** — pulling each chapter's load-bearing idea back out of memory under mixed, no-warning conditions, which is exactly how the exam (and the wilds) will ask for it.

Why a checkpoint at all? Because the single most reliable way to *keep* what you have learned is to **retrieve it after a gap**, interleaved with other material so your brain has to first decide *which tool this is* before applying it. Re-reading feels productive and isn't; cold mixed retrieval feels hard and is. Tonight is built to feel hard.

## The Act I Mind-Map — Six Ideas, One Road

Before the cards, fix the *shape* of the act in your head. Act I is **six ideas in TIA order**, and they are not six islands — they *depend* on each other. The axioms (ch01) make probability legal; conditioning (ch02) reasons within it; a random variable's moments (ch03) summarize a whole distribution; counting (ch04) builds the distributions; the named distributions (ch05) are the count-and-wait models you'll meet again and again; and a deductible (ch06) is just a *transform* of one of those discrete loss variables. Every later piece is built from the earlier ones.

## The 60-Second Retrieval Grid

Cover the right column. For each chapter, say the **one load-bearing idea** aloud before you read it. This should take about a minute total — it is a *warm-up*, not a quiz. Any cell that feels foggy is a chapter to revisit *before* the twelve cards, not after.

| Leg | Chapter | The one idea you must own cold |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **ch01 · Fundamentals** *(A.1)* | Probability is **weighted counting** under three axioms; the **complement** $P(A^c)=1-P(A)$ powers the "at least one" trick, and **inclusion–exclusion** $P(A\cup B)=P(A)+P(B)-P(A\cap B)$ stops you double-counting overlaps. |
| 2 | **ch02 · Conditional & Bayes** *(A.2)* | $P(A\mid B)=\dfrac{P(A\cap B)}{P(B)}$. **Total probability** computes an overall rate as a *weighted average of conditional rates*; **Bayes** flips the conditioning, $P(B_i\mid A)=\dfrac{P(B_i)P(A\mid B_i)}{\sum_j P(B_j)P(A\mid B_j)}$. |
| 3 | **ch03 · Discrete Moments** *(A.3)* | Summarize a whole RV: **mean** $E[X]=\sum k\,p(k)$ (a weighted average of *values*, **not** the mode), **variance** $\Var(X)=E[X^2]-(E[X])^2$, and the transform rule $\Var(aX+b)=a^2\Var(X)$ (shift adds nothing, scale **squares**). |
| 4 | **ch04 · Combinatorics** *(A.4)* | **Classify by order & replacement.** $\binom{n}{k}$ counts unordered selections; the **binomial** counts successes *with* replacement, the **hypergeometric** counts them *without*. |
| 5 | **ch05 · Discrete Distributions** *(A.5)* | The named count/wait models: **geometric** (wait for the 1st success, $E=1/p$, **memoryless**), **negative binomial** (wait for the $r$-th), **Poisson** ($P(k)=\dfrac{e^{-\lambda}\lambda^k}{k!}$, $E=\Var=\lambda$), and **Poissons add** ($\lambda_1+\lambda_2$). |
| 6 | **ch06 · Deductibles & Limits** *(A.6)* | A **payment is a transform of a loss**: deductible $(X-d)_+$, limit $X\wedge u$, and the **budget identity** $E[X\wedge u]+E[(X-d)_+]=E[X]$; mind **per-loss vs per-payment**. |

> *Sixty seconds well spent.* If all six cells came back instantly, you are warmed up — deal the cards. If two or more were foggy, that is your signal: flip back to those chapters' Pokédex Entries for five minutes *first*. Retrieval only consolidates what's already (barely) there; it can't rebuild what's gone.

## The Twelve Cards — A Mixed League Qualifier

::: problem-set
**THE RECAP-NIGHT QUALIFIER — twelve mixed cards, no warning, exam difficulty.** These are **not** sorted by chapter. Each card is drawn from somewhere on the Countable Road, and the **first move is always to recognize which tool it is** — that recognition is the skill the checkpoint trains. Work them timed (~6 min each), method-correct, then check the **Answers**. The **pass gate** follows the answers.

Markers: 🔴 routine method · 🟡 routine-with-a-twist · 🔵 stretch. The small tag after each card names its **source chapter** *(hidden on a real exam — peek only when checking)*.

**Q7.1.** 🔴 Among the campers, $P(\text{Potion})=0.6$, $P(\text{Repel})=0.5$, $P(\text{both})=0.3$. Find (a) $P(\text{at least one})$ and (b) $P(\text{neither})$. *(ch01)*

**Q7.2.** 🟡 $10\%$ of route Pokémon are poisoned. A scanner flags a poisoned one $90\%$ of the time and false-flags a healthy one $20\%$ of the time. A Pokémon is **flagged**. Find $P(\text{poisoned}\mid\text{flagged})$. *(ch02)*

**Q7.3.** 🟡 *(AUDIT — Meowth's note.)* A prize machine's payout $W\in\{0,1,2,3,4\}$ has pmf $0.30,0.25,0.20,0.15,0.10$. Meowth writes "the average payout is **0 tokens** — that's the most common result, so it's the average." Find the true $E[W]$ and name his error. *(ch03)*

**Q7.4.** 🟡 Each flushed Pidgey is independently shiny w.p. $0.2$. Among $n=6$, let $X$ = the number shiny. Find $P(X=2)$. *(ch04)*

**Q7.5.** 🟡 A bag holds $4$ rare and $8$ common balls ($12$ total). You draw $3$ at random **without replacement**. Find $P(\text{exactly 2 rare})$. *(ch04)*

**Q7.6.** 🔵 *(RIVAL TRAP — Gary's boast.)* Each throw catches the roaming Pokémon independently w.p. $p=0.25$. Let $X$ = the number of throws **up to and including** the first catch. Gary says "the average catch takes $3$ throws." Find $E[X]$ and say whether he's right. *(ch05)*

**Q7.7.** 🔴 Wild Pokémon wander into camp as a Poisson process at $\lambda=2$ per hour; $N$ = the number in one hour. Find $P(N=0)$ and $E[N]$. *(ch05)*

**Q7.8.** 🟡 *(AUDIT — the logbook clerk.)* Camp A logs Poisson arrivals at $\lambda_A=1.5/\text{hr}$, camp B independently at $\lambda_B=2.5/\text{hr}$. The clerk writes "combined rate $=1.5\times2.5=3.75$." Give the **true** combined distribution and rate, then find $P(0\text{ combined arrivals in an hour})$. *(ch05)*

**Q7.9.** 🟡 Each of $n=10$ Poké Balls is independently faulty w.p. $p=0.1$; $X$ = the number faulty. Find $E[X]$ and $\Var(X)$. *(ch03 + ch04)*

**Q7.10.** 🔵 Repair loss $X\in\{0,1,2,3,4\}$ (in \$100s) has pmf $0.3,0.3,0.2,0.1,0.1$. A policy carries an ordinary deductible $d=1$, paying $(X-1)_+$ per loss. Find the expected payment per loss, $E[(X-1)_+]$. *(ch06)*

**Q7.11.** 🔵 For the **same** loss $X$ ($0.3,0.3,0.2,0.1,0.1$ on $0$–$4$): (a) find $E[X]$; (b) find the limited expected value $E[X\wedge 2]$; (c) verify the budget identity $E[X\wedge 2]+E[(X-2)_+]=E[X]$. *(ch06)*

**Q7.12.** 🔵 *(DECISION — same odds, different framing.)* Each encounter is an independent catch w.p. $p=0.3$. (a) If you throw **until your first catch**, find the expected number of throws. (b) If you instead make **exactly $4$** throws, find the expected number of catches and $P(\text{at least one catch})$. Which framing do you use if you only want **one** catch as fast as possible? *(ch04 + ch05)*

> *Twelve cards down. Score yourself against the Answers, then read the pass gate. Honesty here is the whole point — a card you "sort of" got is a card to count as missed.*
:::

## Answers

::: answer-key
**Full worked solution per card, with the source chapter and the Pokédex Entry / idea it tests. A quick-answer table opens the section; the pass gate closes it.**

### Quick-Answer Table

| # | Answer | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Q7.1 | (a) $0.8$, (b) $0.2$ | ch01 |
| Q7.2 | $1/3\approx0.333$ | ch02 |
| Q7.3 | $E[W]=1.5$ (mode $\ne$ mean) | ch03 (audit) |
| Q7.4 | $0.24576$ | ch04 (binomial) |
| Q7.5 | $12/55\approx0.2182$ | ch04 (hypergeometric) |
| Q7.6 | $E[X]=4$ (Gary's $3$ omits the catching throw) | ch05 (rival_trap) |
| Q7.7 | $P(N{=}0)=e^{-2}\approx0.1353$, $E[N]=2$ | ch05 (Poisson) |
| Q7.8 | Poisson$(4.0)$; $P(0)=e^{-4}\approx0.0183$ | ch05 (audit) |
| Q7.9 | $E[X]=1.0$, $\Var(X)=0.9$ | ch03+ch04 |
| Q7.10 | $0.7$ (\$100s) | ch06 |
| Q7.11 | $E[X]=1.4$, $E[X\wedge2]=1.1$, $E[(X{-}2)_+]=0.3$ | ch06 |
| Q7.12 | (a) $1/0.3\approx3.333$; (b) $E=1.2$, $P(\ge1)=0.7599$; use geometric | ch04+ch05 (decision) |

**Q7.1** — *(ch01 · complement + inclusion–exclusion.)* (a) $P(P\cup R)=P(P)+P(R)-P(P\cap R)=0.6+0.5-0.3=0.8$. (b) "Neither" is the complement of "at least one": $P(\text{neither})=1-0.8=0.2$. *Recognition cue: overlapping events $\to$ inclusion–exclusion; "neither / none / at least one" $\to$ complement.*

**Q7.2** — *(ch02 · total probability + Bayes.)* Denominator first (total probability): $P(\text{flag})=(0.10)(0.90)+(0.90)(0.20)=0.09+0.18=0.27$. Then Bayes flips the conditioning: $P(\text{poisoned}\mid\text{flag})=\dfrac{0.09}{0.27}=\dfrac{1}{3}\approx0.333$. *The base rate ($10\%$) is low, so even a good scanner leaves you only $\sim33\%$ sure — the false-positive lesson.*

**Q7.3** — *(ch03 · audit, mode-as-mean.)* True mean: $E[W]=0(0.30)+1(0.25)+2(0.20)+3(0.15)+4(0.10)=0+0.25+0.40+0.45+0.40=1.5$. **Meowth's error:** he reported the **mode** ($0$, the largest mass) as the **mean.** The most *common* payout is not the *average* payout — the upper values $1$–$4$ drag the mean up to $1.5$. *(The recycled Act-I Team-Rocket trap: never quote the tallest bar when asked for the average.)*

**Q7.4** — *(ch04 · binomial, with replacement.)* Each Pidgey is an independent success/failure trial, so $X\sim\text{Binomial}(6,0.2)$: $P(X=2)=\binom{6}{2}(0.2)^2(0.8)^4=15(0.04)(0.4096)=0.24576$. *Recognition cue: a fixed number of independent trials, each success w.p. $p$ $\to$ binomial.*

**Q7.5** — *(ch04 · hypergeometric, without replacement.)* Drawing without replacement from a finite bag is hypergeometric: $P(\text{2 rare})=\dfrac{\binom{4}{2}\binom{8}{1}}{\binom{12}{3}}=\dfrac{6\cdot8}{220}=\dfrac{48}{220}=\dfrac{12}{55}\approx0.2182$. *The "without replacement" wording is the tell — a binomial would over-state it because the draws aren't independent.*

**Q7.6** — *(ch05 · rival_trap, geometric mean.)* $X$ counts throws **up to and including** the first catch, so $X\sim\text{Geometric}(p)$ in *trials* form: $E[X]=\dfrac{1}{p}=\dfrac{1}{0.25}=4$. **Gary is wrong:** the average is $4$ throws, not $3$. His $3$ is the *failures-before-success* mean $\dfrac{1-p}{p}=3$, which counts only the misses and forgets the throw that actually lands the catch. *Always check which geometric convention a problem wants — "including the success" $\to$ $1/p$.*

**Q7.7** — *(ch05 · Poisson.)* $N\sim\text{Poisson}(2)$: $P(N=0)=e^{-2}\approx0.1353$, and the Poisson mean is the rate, $E[N]=\lambda=2$. *Recognition cue: "events arriving at a rate $\lambda$ per interval, count in one interval" $\to$ Poisson; $P(0)=e^{-\lambda}$.*

**Q7.8** — *(ch05 · audit, Poisson superposition.)* Independent Poisson streams **add** (superposition), so the combined count is $\text{Poisson}(\lambda_A+\lambda_B)=\text{Poisson}(1.5+2.5)=\text{Poisson}(4.0)$ — **not** the product. The clerk multiplied the rates instead of adding them. $P(0)=e^{-4.0}\approx0.0183$. *Rates add; only independent MGFs multiply.*

**Q7.9** — *(ch03 + ch04 · moments of a binomial.)* $X\sim\text{Binomial}(10,0.1)$, so $E[X]=np=10(0.1)=1.0$ and $\Var(X)=np(1-p)=10(0.1)(0.9)=0.9$. *This card fuses two legs: ch04 names the distribution, ch03's machinery hands you its mean and variance from the formulas.*

**Q7.10** — *(ch06 · ordinary deductible.)* The per-loss payment $(X-1)_+$ takes values $0,0,1,2,3$ on $X=0,1,2,3,4$. Weight by the pmf: $E[(X-1)_+]=0(0.3)+0(0.3)+1(0.2)+2(0.1)+3(0.1)=0.2+0.2+0.3=0.7$ (\$100s). *A deductible truncates the payment below $d$ to zero, then you take the ordinary expectation of the truncated values.*

**Q7.11** — *(ch06 · limit + budget identity.)* (a) $E[X]=0(0.3)+1(0.3)+2(0.2)+3(0.1)+4(0.1)=0.3+0.4+0.3+0.4=1.4$. (b) $X\wedge2=\min(X,2)$ takes values $0,1,2,2,2$, so $E[X\wedge2]=0(0.3)+1(0.3)+2(0.2)+2(0.1)+2(0.1)=0.3+0.4+0.2+0.2=1.1$. (c) $(X-2)_+$ takes values $0,0,0,1,2$, so $E[(X-2)_+]=1(0.1)+2(0.1)=0.3$, and the budget identity holds: $1.1+0.3=1.4=E[X]$. ✓ *The limit keeps the bottom layer, the deductible-excess keeps the top layer, and the two always reassemble $E[X]$.*

**Q7.12** — *(ch04 + ch05 · decision, geometric vs binomial framing.)* (a) "Throw until the first catch" is geometric in trials form: $E=\dfrac{1}{p}=\dfrac{1}{0.3}\approx3.333$ throws. (b) "Exactly $4$ throws" is binomial: $E[\text{catches}]=np=4(0.3)=1.2$, and $P(\text{at least one})=1-(1-p)^4=1-0.7^4=1-0.2401=0.7599$. **Decision:** if you only want **one** catch *as fast as possible*, use the **geometric "throw until first catch"** framing — you stop the instant you succeed ($\sim3.33$ throws on average) instead of committing to a fixed batch of $4$. *Same per-attempt odds, two different questions: "how long until success" (geometric) vs. "how many in a fixed batch" (binomial).*

## The Pass Gate

::: concept-gate
**CHECKPOINT A — PASS GATE**

Count the cards you solved **with the correct method** (a lucky number with broken reasoning does not count).

- **10, 11, or 12 / 12 — QUALIFIED. ✅** Act I is a set of reflexes, not notes. Bank the campfire, douse the kettle, and cross into Act II — the **smooth, continuous wilds**, where probability stops stacking on whole numbers and starts to *flow*. Your discrete instincts transfer: a sum becomes an integral, a pmf becomes a density, but $E[X]$, $\Var(X)$, conditioning, and the deductible transforms are the *same ideas* in a new coat.

- **Below 10 / 12 — NOT YET. Camp here and run the highlights again.** This is the **spaced-retrieval fail path**, and it is a feature, not a setback: crossing into the continuous world with a shaky discrete foundation just moves the failure two chapters downstream, where it costs more. Do this:
  1. **Diagnose by source chapter.** Use the source tag on each missed card to find the leg that's leaking. (Two misses both tagged ch05? The named distributions are the gap.)
  2. **Revisit, don't re-read.** Reopen only the leaking chapters' **Pokédex Entries** and **Trainer's Tips** — the compressed cores, not the full nine-beat lessons — and re-work that chapter's **Gym Battle** problem from scratch.
  3. **Retry in 2 days.** Space the retry by about **48 hours** (not the same night). The gap is what makes the retrieval stick; cramming the retry immediately rebuilds short-term familiarity that evaporates by Act II. Re-run all twelve cards cold.

*Either way, the standard is the same: you cross the bridge fluent, or you don't cross it yet.*

## From Kanto to the Real World

::: kanto-realworld
**⬛ FROM KANTO TO THE REAL WORLD**

A mixed checkpoint mirrors **how actuarial work actually arrives.** A claims file does not come stamped "this is a Poisson frequency problem" or "this is a Bayesian credibility problem"; the practitioner must **recognize the structure** before reaching for a tool — exactly the skill tonight's no-warning cards train. The six Act-I ideas are the discrete backbone of pricing: **inclusion–exclusion** (ch01) keeps overlapping coverages from being double-counted; **Bayes** (ch02) is the seed of experience rating and fraud detection; **$E[X]$ and $\Var(X)$** (ch03) are the pure premium and the risk load on every policy; **counting** (ch04) gives the selection odds in underwriting and reinsurance; the **Poisson and friends** (ch05) are the standard **claim-frequency** models; and **deductibles, limits, and the budget identity** (ch06) are the literal arithmetic of how a policy turns a loss into a payment.

*Series bridge:* the **spaced, interleaved retrieval** you just practised is also the evidence-based way to study for any of the SOA/CAS exams — re-working mixed problems after a gap beats re-reading every time. Act II rebuilds all six of these ideas in the continuous world (densities, integrals, the exponential and normal), so the firmer your discrete reflexes are tonight, the lighter the crossing will feel.

*Transfer check:* with no Pokémon in any of it — "given two overlapping events find the union; invert a diagnostic test with Bayes; find the mean and variance of a Binomial$(10,0.1)$; combine two independent Poisson streams; price a deductible on a discrete loss" — could you still recognize and solve each cold? If yes, Act I has transferred, and that is the only thing this checkpoint was ever measuring.
:::
